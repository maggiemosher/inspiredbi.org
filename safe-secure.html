Legal Compliance

Privacy Laws Every Educator Must Know

Before adopting any AI tool in an educational setting, educators must understand the three federal privacy laws that govern student and patient data. Violations can result in loss of federal funding, lawsuits, and serious harm to students.

FERPA

Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act

FERPA protects the privacy of student education records for any institution receiving federal funding. Under FERPA, schools may not disclose personally identifiable information (PII) from education records without written consent of the parent or eligible student (18+).

Key rules for AI use:

  • Never paste student names, ID numbers, grades, or behavioral records into a public AI tool (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini free tier)
  • AI tools used with student data must have a FERPA-compliant Business Associate Agreement or School Official designation
  • Aggregated, de-identified data is generally permissible — but verify with your legal counsel
  • Google Workspace for Education (Fundamentals and above) is FERPA compliant when used within the designated core services
  • Parents have the right to review and challenge records — including AI-generated documents if they become part of the official record
HIPAA

Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act

HIPAA protects individually identifiable health information (PHI). While HIPAA primarily applies to healthcare providers, it intersects with education when schools provide health services, maintain health records, or when counselors and school psychologists store student health information.

Key rules for AI use:

  • Never input student mental health information, IEP health data, or medical accommodations into non-HIPAA-compliant AI tools
  • School nurses, counselors, and psychologists using AI for case notes need HIPAA-compliant platforms
  • Most general AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, standard Gemini) are NOT HIPAA compliant
  • HIPAA and FERPA interact in complex ways for school health records — consult your district's legal counsel for school-specific guidance
COPPA

Children's Online Privacy Protection Act

COPPA restricts the collection of personal information from children under 13 by online services. It requires verifiable parental consent before collecting, using, or disclosing personal information from children under 13.

Key rules for AI use:

  • Schools deploying AI tools for students under 13 must verify the tool is COPPA compliant or has an educational exception
  • Under COPPA's school exception, schools can consent on behalf of parents for educational purposes — but must be able to demonstrate the tool's data use is for educational purposes only
  • The tool must not use student data for advertising, share it with third parties, or retain it longer than necessary
  • Verified COPPA-compliant tools for K–8: Google Workspace for Education, Mizou, SchoolAI, Khanmigo, Diffit
  • Always check the platform's current privacy policy — compliance status can change with product updates

IT Department Recommendations

Recommendations for IT administrators evaluating AI platform deployment across K–12 and university environments.

RECOMMENDED · LOW RISK

Google Workspace for Education

Best-in-class for K–12 and higher ed due to strong FERPA/COPPA compliance, centralized admin controls, no student advertising, and the ability to disable AI features at the organizational unit level. Education Fundamentals is free. Gemini features require Education Plus or higher. Agentic AI requires Google AI Pro for Education and IT configuration of Workspace Studio.

RECOMMENDED · LOW RISK

Microsoft 365 Education

Strong FERPA/COPPA compliance through A1–A5 licensing. Microsoft Copilot for Education integrates with Word, Teams, and OneNote. Excellent for institutions already standardized on Microsoft. Copilot Studio enables custom agent building. FedRAMP High certified — suitable for sensitive research environments. A1 tier is free for schools.

RECOMMENDED · LOW RISK

Apple Education (Apple Intelligence)

On-device AI processing through Apple Intelligence provides the strongest privacy posture — student data never leaves the device for most AI features. Apple Business Manager provides centralized management. Best suited for 1:1 iPad or Mac programs. No advertising, no behavioral tracking. Limited agentic capabilities compared to Google or Microsoft.

CONDITIONAL · MEDIUM RISK (K–12) · LOW RISK (University with agreement)

Anthropic Claude

Claude Pro ($20/mo) is appropriate for individual educator use with no student PII. For institutional deployment with students, require an Enterprise or API agreement with a Data Processing Addendum that addresses FERPA. Claude's free tier should be treated as a consumer product — do not use with student data. Claude Cowork (desktop, paid plan) is appropriate for teacher productivity with non-student data.

IT Deployment Checklist

  • Before deploying any AI tool, obtain and review the vendor's Data Processing Agreement (DPA)
  • Verify the tool's FERPA compliance documentation — ask for it in writing
  • Confirm the tool does NOT use student data to train its AI models
  • Test admin controls: can you disable specific AI features for student accounts?
  • Determine if parental consent is required for students under 13 (COPPA)
  • Create a documented AI tool approval process — staff should not self-adopt without IT review
  • Establish an AI incident response plan for data exposure events

Pedagogical Guide: What Teachers Should Actually Monitor

Monitoring AI use isn't about catching cheating — it's about understanding how students think. Here are the three highest-value behaviors to observe in student AI logs:

Prompt Iteration

Does the student refine their prompt after receiving an unsatisfactory response? This is the most reliable indicator of AI literacy. A student who sends one prompt and accepts the response passively is at cognitive risk. A student who argues with the AI, challenges its output, or refines their question is developing critical thinking skills. Look for: multiple turns, directional language ("that's not right, try again with..."), and self-correction.

Fact-Checking & Verification

Do students treat AI output as authoritative, or do they attempt to verify claims? Monitor whether students ask the AI for sources, cross-reference with uploaded materials, or flag responses as uncertain. Instructional move: Build a "Verify Before You Trust" step into all AI-assisted assignments.

Cognitive Offloading vs. Cognitive Scaffolding

Cognitive offloading = using AI to bypass thinking entirely (asking it to write the essay). Cognitive scaffolding = using AI to support thinking (asking it to explain a concept, then writing your own response). Conversation logs quickly reveal which is happening. Design your assignments and AI environments to make scaffolding the only possible use case — configure the AI to refuse direct answer delivery and redirect to guiding questions.

Cyber Security Framework

The Six Domains of Cyber Security

Modern cyber security isn't one thing — it's a layered system of six interconnected domains, each protecting a different part of your digital environment. Understanding how these pieces fit together helps educators, IT leaders, and administrators make smarter decisions when adopting AI tools and managing school technology.

The six domains of cyber security and the key practices within each.

Endpoint Security

Protecting the Devices People Use

An "endpoint" is any device that connects to your network — a Chromebook, iPad, teacher laptop, or smartphone. Endpoint security ensures that every device accessing school systems is trusted, managed, and protected against compromise. This is the first line of defense because attackers frequently target the weakest device to gain entry to a larger network.

What the practices mean:

  • Device Authentication — Only registered, verified devices are allowed to connect. Prevents unknown devices (like a student's personal phone) from accessing school systems without approval.
  • Network Segmentation — Student devices, staff devices, and administrative systems are kept on separate network segments so a compromised student device cannot reach payroll or student records.
  • Firmware Updates — Keeping the low-level software inside devices (below the operating system) updated to patch known vulnerabilities. Often overlooked but critical.
  • Encryption for IoT Data — Smart devices (thermostats, door sensors, security cameras) transmit data that must be encrypted so it cannot be intercepted.
  • Anomaly Detection — Software that notices when a device is behaving unusually (sending abnormal amounts of data, connecting to unexpected servers) and raises an alert.
  • Vulnerability Management — A continuous process of scanning devices for known weaknesses and patching them before attackers can exploit them.
  • Device Lifecycle Management — Policies for what happens when a device is lost, retired, or reassigned — including remote wiping and data removal.
  • Secure Boot — A hardware feature that ensures a device only loads trusted, verified software when it starts up, preventing malicious code from embedding itself at the deepest level.
Network Security

Protecting Data as It Travels

Network security covers everything that happens as data moves from one place to another — through school Wi-Fi, the internet, or between cloud services. If endpoint security protects devices, network security protects the roads those devices use to communicate. In an AI-heavy environment, virtually all interactions pass through network infrastructure, making this domain especially critical.

What the practices mean:

  • Intrusion Detection System (IDS) — Monitors network traffic and flags suspicious patterns, like repeated failed login attempts or unusual data transfers. It watches but does not block.
  • Intrusion Prevention System (IPS) — Like an IDS, but it actively blocks detected threats in real time rather than just alerting administrators.
  • Network Access Control (NAC) — A gatekeeper system that checks each device's health and identity before allowing it onto the network. Unpatched or unrecognized devices can be quarantined automatically.
  • SSL/TLS (Transport Layer Security) — The protocol that encrypts data in transit. When you see "https://" in a URL, TLS is protecting the data between your browser and the server. All reputable AI platforms use TLS.
  • Virtual Private Network (VPN) — Creates an encrypted tunnel between a remote user and the school network. Important for staff working from home who need access to sensitive systems.
  • Firewalls — Hardware or software barriers that filter incoming and outgoing network traffic based on rules. Schools commonly use firewalls to block access to harmful websites and to control which external services are reachable.
  • Traffic Analysis and Anomaly Detection — Continuous analysis of network traffic patterns to identify unusual behavior — such as a device suddenly uploading gigabytes of data to an unknown server — that could indicate a breach or data exfiltration.
IoT Security

Protecting Smart Devices and Connected Sensors

IoT (Internet of Things) refers to the growing ecosystem of internet-connected physical devices beyond traditional computers — smart TVs, security cameras, HVAC sensors, badge readers, and even AI-powered classroom tools. These devices are notoriously difficult to secure because they often lack robust operating systems, receive infrequent updates, and outnumber traditional computers on school networks by a wide margin.

What the practices mean:

  • Device Authentication — Each IoT device must prove its identity before communicating on the network, preventing rogue devices from masquerading as legitimate ones.
  • Network Segmentation for IoT — Placing all IoT devices on their own isolated network segment so that a compromised smart thermostat cannot provide a path into the student records database.
  • Firmware Updates — Automatically pushing security patches to IoT devices, which often have vulnerabilities discovered months or years after deployment.
  • Encryption for IoT Data — Ensuring data collected by sensors (temperature, motion, access logs) is encrypted both in transit and at rest, even on low-power devices.
  • Anomaly Detection — Monitoring IoT device behavior for sudden changes (a camera that starts sending data at 3 a.m. to an overseas IP address) that may indicate compromise.
  • Vulnerability Management — Tracking known vulnerabilities in each IoT device model and prioritizing patches or device replacement when critical flaws are discovered.
  • Device Lifecycle Management — Procedures for securely decommissioning IoT devices — resetting them to factory defaults and removing them from network access lists when they are retired.
  • Secure Boot — Verifying that IoT device firmware has not been tampered with when the device powers on.
Application Security

Protecting the Software Educators and Students Use

Application security (AppSec) focuses on making the software itself — learning management systems, AI tools, student portals, and custom-built apps — resistant to attack. Most data breaches in education involve exploiting vulnerabilities in web applications. When evaluating AI tools for school use, AppSec practices are a key indicator of whether a vendor takes security seriously.

What the practices mean:

  • Secure Code Review — Human experts examine the application's source code looking for security flaws before the software is released. A vendor that does code reviews is less likely to ship vulnerabilities.
  • Web Application Firewall (WAF) — A filter that sits in front of a web application and blocks common attack patterns like SQL injection and cross-site scripting (XSS) before they reach the application.
  • API Security — APIs are how applications talk to each other (how SchoolAI talks to its AI model, for example). API security ensures those connections are authenticated, encrypted, and rate-limited so they cannot be abused.
  • Runtime Application Self-Protection (RASP) — Security controls built directly into an application that can detect and block attacks as they happen, even when a WAF misses them.
  • Software Composition Analysis (SCA) — Automated scanning of the third-party libraries and components an application uses to identify known vulnerabilities in dependencies. Important because most modern apps rely heavily on open-source code.
  • Secure Software Development Life Cycle (SSDLC) — A development process where security requirements, threat modeling, code review, and penetration testing are built into every phase of building an application — not bolted on at the end.
  • Static Application Security Testing (SAST) — Automated tools that scan source code for vulnerabilities without running the application. Finds issues early and inexpensively.
  • Dynamic Application Security Testing (DAST) — Automated tools that test a running application by simulating real attacks. Finds issues that only appear when the software is actually executing.
Cloud Security

Protecting Data Stored and Processed in the Cloud

Nearly every AI tool used in education today is a cloud service — the AI model, the data storage, and the application layer all run on cloud infrastructure. Cloud security ensures that student data stored in these environments is protected, access is controlled, and the shared responsibility between your institution and the cloud provider is clearly understood.

What the practices mean:

  • Cloud Access Security Broker (CASB) — A security layer between your users and cloud services that enforces security policies, monitors usage, and prevents unauthorized data sharing. Large districts use CASBs to see which cloud apps staff are using and block unapproved ones.
  • Data Encryption — All data stored in the cloud should be encrypted at rest (when stored) and in transit (when moving). Top-tier vendors use AES-256 encryption — ask vendors for their encryption specifications.
  • Identity and Access Management (IAM) — Controls who can access which cloud resources. Strong IAM means using single sign-on (SSO), multi-factor authentication (MFA), and least-privilege access so users can only see data relevant to their role.
  • Security Posture Management — Continuously assessing cloud environment configurations against security best practices and flagging misconfigurations — like a storage bucket accidentally made publicly accessible — before they become breaches.
  • Shared Responsibility Model — Cloud providers secure the infrastructure; you secure your data and how you configure the service. Understanding where the provider's responsibility ends and yours begins is critical. Many breaches happen in the gap.
  • Continuous Compliance Monitoring — Automated checks that your cloud environment remains compliant with FERPA, HIPAA, or other regulations 24/7, not just at annual audit time.
  • Cloud Identity Federation — Connecting your school's identity system (Google Workspace, Microsoft Entra ID) to cloud services so students and staff use one set of credentials across all approved tools.
  • Cloud Security Audits — Periodic third-party reviews of your cloud configurations, access logs, and security controls. Look for vendors who share their audit results (SOC 2 Type II reports are the standard).
Data Security

Protecting the Data Itself — Wherever It Lives

Data security focuses on protecting the actual information — student records, grades, health data, personally identifiable information (PII) — regardless of where it is stored or how it is accessed. Unlike network or endpoint security, which protect containers and pathways, data security treats the data itself as the primary asset to be protected. In education, this directly maps to FERPA, HIPAA, and COPPA compliance.

What the practices mean:

  • Encryption (At Rest & In Transit) — Data is encrypted both when stored on servers (at rest) and while being transmitted between systems (in transit). Both are required for FERPA-compliant AI tools. Encryption ensures that even if data is stolen, it is unreadable without the decryption key.
  • Data Masking — Replacing sensitive data values with realistic but fictional substitutes for use in testing, analytics, or demonstrations. A report can show "Student A" instead of a real name and still be useful for analysis — protecting privacy without losing utility.
  • Data Loss Prevention (DLP) — Policies and software that detect and block the unauthorized transmission of sensitive data. A DLP system can, for example, prevent a teacher from emailing a spreadsheet of student Social Security numbers or pasting PII into an unapproved AI tool.
  • Access Controls — Strict rules determining who can read, modify, or delete specific data. A classroom teacher should be able to see their own students' grades, but not another teacher's roster. Role-based access controls implement this at scale.
  • Backup and Recovery — Regular, tested backups of critical data so that a ransomware attack or accidental deletion does not result in permanent data loss. The test is essential — backups that have never been restored are often discovered to be corrupt when they are most needed.
  • Data Integrity Verification — Mechanisms (like cryptographic hashes) that detect whether data has been tampered with. Ensures that a student's transcript, when accessed, is exactly what was originally stored.
  • Tokenization — Replacing sensitive data (like a student ID number) with a non-sensitive token that can be used in systems without exposing the real value. The token is meaningless to an attacker; only the tokenization system can map it back to the original.
  • IoT Security for Data — Applying data security principles specifically to the streams of information generated by connected devices — access logs, sensor readings, and usage data — which are increasingly used in smart campus and classroom management systems.

What This Means for Educators Adopting AI Tools

  • Ask AI vendors which of these six domains their security program covers — a mature vendor will have documentation for all six.
  • SOC 2 Type II reports are the standard proof that a vendor's security controls across these domains have been independently audited. Request this report before deploying any tool with student data.
  • The Shared Responsibility Model means your school has obligations too — enabling MFA, using approved apps, and not pasting student PII into unapproved AI tools are your side of the security equation.
  • For K–12, focus first on Data Security (FERPA/COPPA) and Cloud Security (where most EdTech lives). Endpoint and Application Security are primarily the vendor's domain — audit them by reviewing their certifications.
  • Network Security is largely your IT department's responsibility. The most impactful step educators can take is using only approved, district-managed devices on school networks when accessing student data.
