K-12 Educators

Personalized learning tools expand

What's New · July 2026

Classroom AI just got more personalized — and more supervised. Fresh from ISTE 2026, Google confirmed that Guided Learning in Gemini is rolling out across Workspace for Education this month, walking students through problems step-by-step instead of handing over answers, while a new Class Tools feature lets teachers lock student screens into Guided Learning mode during class to cut distractions without losing visibility into student work. Read Along, a real-time literacy support tool, is now available across all Workspace for Education editions. On the ground, districts like Albuquerque Public Schools are already using Gemini and tools like Brisk to identify learning gaps and flag struggling students for reteaching. The catch: training hasn't kept pace with rollout. EdTech Magazine reports just 18% of K-12 teachers have received formal guidance on how AI should be used in their own work, even as EdTech surveys suggest AI tool use in classrooms has become common practice. The lesson for this week: adopt the tools, but pair every rollout with explicit teacher training.

School Leaders

Ohio's AI mandate takes effect

What's New · July 2026

The deadline everyone was watching has now passed. As of July 1, 2026, every public, community, and STEM school in Ohio is legally required to have adopted a formal AI use policy under House Bill 96 — either the state's model policy or a locally developed one aligned with it. Columbus City Schools got ahead of the mandate, with its board unanimously adopting a policy that treats AI as a learning supplement, not a substitute for student effort, giving teachers full discretion over assignment-level AI use and treating unauthorized use as plagiarism. Nationally, momentum is following Ohio's lead: nearly 80% of districts now report established AI guidelines, up sharply from just 57% having any guidance in 2025. NASBE says the center of gravity for 2026 has officially shifted from writing guidance to enforcing it — with safety, equity, and workforce readiness as the new focus areas. California's AI working group is expected to issue its own policy recommendations by the end of this month. For leaders still without a policy: the clock is no longer theoretical.

Higher Education

Global AI adoption hits a tipping point

What's New · July 2026

The largest study of its kind confirms what campus leaders have suspected for a year: AI use in higher education is no longer emerging, it's default. The Digital Education Council's 2026 Global Survey drew 45,398 responses — 27,284 students and 18,114 faculty — across 35 countries, the most geographically diverse dataset on AI adoption in higher ed ever assembled; a companion LATAM survey found 92% of students and 79% of faculty actively engaging with AI. Institutional deployment is scaling to match: OpenAI and the California State University system announced access to ChatGPT Edu for 460,000+ students and 63,000+ faculty and staff, the largest organization-wide AI deployment to date. Gallup separately confirmed 57% of U.S. college students use AI in coursework at least weekly. But gaps remain stark: only 15% of students say AI is well-integrated into their courses, and just 51% of graduates feel adequately AI-skilled for employment — while AI-linked academic misconduct keeps rising as legacy detection tools fall behind. For KU and peer institutions, the strategic question has shifted from "should we adopt AI" to "how do we close the integration gap."

K-12 Educators

AI literacy & teacher productivity tools

What's New · June 2026

Teachers are now AI's biggest believers. A new national poll reported by NPR (June 5) found that most K-12 teachers say AI's impact on education will eclipse the internet or computers, with roughly 6 in 10 teachers having already used AI to help with work tasks. Yet student classroom use lags — more than half of teachers say students aren't using AI in class at all. The biggest policy news: New York City Public Schools released preliminary AI guidance for its 1.1 million students, permitting teachers to use AI for brainstorming, drafting communications, and lesson planning, while prohibiting its use for assigning grades, disciplinary decisions, or collecting biometric data — a comprehensive Playbook is due this month. On the training front, Google partnered with ISTE+ASCD to launch a free, standards-aligned AI Educator Series (available from May 13) covering tools like Gemini and NotebookLM. The gap remains real: a CDT report found under half of teachers and students have received any AI training from their institutions. The takeaway for classrooms: AI literacy and responsible-use instruction can't wait.

School Leaders

Policy deadlines & AI governance

What's New · June 2026

The deadline is here. Under Ohio's House Bill 96, every school district must adopt a formal AI use policy no later than July 1, 2026 — districts are encouraged to form AI workgroups focused on ethical use, data privacy, academic integrity, and staff training. The broader signal from the field: districts are prioritizing AI governance over adoption speed (GovTech), and leaders increasingly say AI strategy can no longer be delegated to IT — superintendents must be personally proximate to the decision-making to align AI with organizational goals. A standout model: this July, AASA, nonprofit Day of AI, and MIT RAISE will host a three-day Boston workshop where students themselves lead the drafting of a model AI policy any district can adopt — directly tackling the #1 barrier leaders cite: lack of clear policy and professional development. State guidance keeps maturing too, with Michigan's MDE releasing comprehensive district AI guidance on May 12. The winning move now: stand up a cross-functional governance team — teachers, IT, parents, board, and student voice — before mandates force the issue.

Higher Education

Rethinking assessment in the AI era

What's New · June 2026

Student AI use has become routine — and assessment must catch up. The Lumina Foundation–Gallup 2026 State of Higher Education study found more than half (57%) of U.S. college students use AI in their coursework at least weekly, with about 1 in 5 using it daily; business, technology, and engineering majors are the heaviest users. Stanford HAI's 2026 AI Index confirms AI use is now "near universal" among undergraduates. The pressure point is integrity: a global synthesis published in March 2026 found near-universal adoption alongside a sharp rise in misconduct, with AI-related violations at the University of Pennsylvania increasing sevenfold across consecutive years as legacy plagiarism detectors fail. Cornell researchers (May) argue plainly that widespread AI misuse means higher ed must rethink assessment itself. Forward-leaning institutions are responding: Washington State University moved away from "detect-and-punish" toward "AI-Positive" syllabus statements with three standardized tiers — AI-Required, AI-Assisted, and No-AI. EDUCAUSE and Frontiers in Education both identify integrated AI governance — faculty training, resourcing, and transparent guidelines — as the critical gap most institutions still must close. For KU, the message is clear: design assessments for an AI-fluent student body.

K-12 Educators

Image generators, media literacy & teaching discernment

What's New · June 2026

AI image generation has reached a tipping point that every classroom teacher needs to understand. A 2026 EdWeek Research Center survey found that 61% of elementary educators report their students struggle significantly to distinguish AI-generated images from real photographs — and with some estimates placing AI-generated content at over 57% of all online images, that challenge is only growing. The newest tools from Adobe Firefly, Midjourney, and Google's ImageFX now embed C2PA provenance metadata directly into AI-generated files, giving teachers a new tool for teaching visual verification. CoSN's 2026 Top Topics in K-12 Innovation has placed Critical AI Media Literacy at the top of its list, meaning districts are beginning to require structured "spot the AI" instruction across grade levels. The best classroom strategy right now: teach students to slow down, ask "how do I know this is real?", use reverse image search, and look for telltale signs like distorted hands, inconsistent backgrounds, and missing metadata. These aren't just tech skills — they are the new foundational literacy.

School Leaders

Policy, governance & district AI strategy

What's New · June 2026

The policy window for school leaders is closing fast. Ohio's House Bill 96 mandates that every school district adopt a formal AI use policy by July 1, 2026 — and similar legislation is moving in 12 additional states. Nationally, the percentage of districts without any AI guidelines has dropped from 43% in 2025 to 21% in 2026 (CoSN), but that still means 1 in 5 districts is operating without guardrails. The most progressive shift: districts that are winning on AI are the ones that moved from the outdated "ban or allow" framing to a third option — teach responsible use through structured, supervised AI literacy embedded across the curriculum. New York City's Department of Education is finalizing its June 2026 AI Playbook, Michigan's MDE released comprehensive district guidance in May, and NASBE has stated plainly that 2026 is the year for enforcement, not just guidance. Leaders who act now — with a cross-functional AI governance team including teachers, parents, IT, and student voice — are the ones who will be ahead of mandates rather than scrambling to comply. Higher Education

Research, governance & institutional strategy

What's New · June 2026

EDUCAUSE's landmark 2026 report, The Impact of AI on Work in Higher Education, confirms what early adopters already know: institutions with proactive AI governance frameworks are pulling ahead on every measurable dimension — faculty productivity, research output quality, student satisfaction, and grant competitiveness. The landmark finding from Stanford HAI's 2026 AI Index: generative AI has moved from novelty to near universality among undergraduates in just three years, with the central question no longer being whether students use AI, but how well institutions are equipping them to use it responsibly. The University of Memphis, Arkansas, Mississippi, and UT Health Science Center launched the Mid-South AI Research Consortium this spring — a model for regional collaboration that research universities like KU should be watching closely. Meanwhile, a global synthesis published in March 2026 found a sharp rise in AI-related academic integrity incidents alongside near-universal student AI use, underscoring the urgency of institutional frameworks that address both ethics and authentic learning. The good news: Northwestern's Kellogg reports that 2,500+ business leaders enrolled in its AI strategy program in the past year — confirming strong market demand for graduates who can lead in AI-integrated organizations. KU graduates with AI fluency have a measurable career advantage.
